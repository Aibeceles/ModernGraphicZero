I confess I don't know much about the student loan debacle, but a friend of mine has a legal specialty in this area. He recently posted the following in response to another friend's posting about her loans.

From Bruce Ralston: Many of my student loan clients find themselves this situation (owing more after paying for years). By far the most common reason is that they put their loans into forbearance rather than getting into the right Income-Driven Repayment plan (IDR). And they often do it repeatedly over the years, not realizing that interest is growing and capitalizing. Their main focus is eating and keeping a roof over their heads, so they either take the forbearance or simply stop paying. Both are mistakes.
The real problem is the servicing agencies (private companies that have contracted with the Dept. of Education to manage these loans). Their mandate is supposed to be to help the borrowers manage the process and stay out of default, but they all do a poor job, with some being worse than others. They not only hide the ball, but they often encourage people to apply for forbearance rather than setting up a proper IDR. And even when they do suggest IDR, they don't ask the right questions, so borrowers often end up in the wrong repayment plan, or they misunderstand the rules about the plan they are in.
Why? Two reasons, both of which boil down to the fact that they are private corporations who see their primary function as making money for their stockholders.
(A) It takes about 10 minutes to set up a forbearance, but it can take 45 - 60 minutes analyze the situation and process the numbers so as to set up the right IDR. Said another way, in the time it takes to set up a proper IDR, they can do 5 or 6 forbearances. They see it as a productivity issue.
(B) They make more money when things go wrong. They get the late fees and part of the added interest, and when the account does do into default the Dept of Ed pays them a bunch of money to take over the account. The system is designed for failure.
In 2016 Ed was putting the finishing touches on a new system that would have simplified and centralized the process. But then the election happened, and the new president put someone in charge whose personal interests were directly contrary to those of the borrowers. The new system was scrapped, and in some ways the old system became even worse. Of course, it's impossible to say how well that new system would have worked, but it certainly showed promise.
Maybe I shouldn't complain. For me it's job security. If the services would simply do their jobs, then the vast majority of my student loan clients wouldn't need to hire me. Frankly, I would rather do other things which are sexier and which pay much better, but as long as the need exists I will try to supply the fix.
But seriously, folks. Just vote. And tell everyone that you know to do the same.
